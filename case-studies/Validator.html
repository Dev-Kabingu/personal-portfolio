Product Design Case Study

Validator

A mobile app and website that help founders check if their idea is worth building — before they spend time and money building it.

Problem Statement

Founders launch on instinct, not evidence.

Early-stage founders in Nairobi, Lagos, and Kampala had no quick, affordable way to check if their idea already had failed competitors, a gap in the market, or customers complaining about a problem worth solving. The research tools out there were either built for other markets, or too slow and expensive for someone just testing an idea. Validator takes a rough idea and gives back a clear, local answer on whether it's worth pursuing — in under 30 seconds.

Design Process

From a detective theme to a product people trust.

The first version treated idea validation like an investigation — digging up clues the way a detective builds a case file. This gave it a "case file" look: stamp red, dark green, brass, and paper textures. It looked unique, but a case-file feel says "you're under suspicion," not "let's build something great." So the whole look was rebuilt to feel cleaner and calmer, and easier to read quickly on a phone.

The case-file look was slow to read on mobile
It felt more like an accusation than encouragement
Paper textures made screens heavier, not clearer
The name described a theme, not what the app does

Key Decisions

Three choices that made a big difference.

Pay after, not before

Founders see free sample results first, then get asked to pay to unlock the full report. This builds trust before asking for money.

SWOT in a simple grid

Strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and strategies are shown in four color-coded boxes, so you can take it all in at a glance.

One score, 0 to 10

Every report gives one simple number, so a founder checking several ideas can compare them side by side.

Reflection

What I learned building Validator.

The final design kept the ambition of the first idea — a tool that feels serious and trustworthy — without the extra weight that made it slow to use on a phone. The biggest lesson: a design can look unique and still be hard to use. Noticing that, and being willing to rebuild instead of defending the first idea, is part of good design work.
